Brand Foundation

Born in Latin America, roasted in Auckland. Everything we make should feel like it comes from the same place — a deep, genuine passion for the world's finest specialty coffee, told with warmth and without pretension.

Mission & story

"To provide the most enjoyable cup of coffee."

Founded by brothers born and raised in Latin America — in the same communities from which most of our coffees are sourced. School trips to coffee farms gave an early exposure to growing, harvesting, and preparing coffee. To this day we source from those same families whenever we can.

Every coffee traceable to farm and farmer. Unique, in-season lots — no exceptions.

Direct trade, above-market prices, and active support for growing communities.
Sustainability

Logo & Wordmark

The Altezano Brothers logo is a framed wordmark — bold condensed type enclosed in a ruled rectangular border. It appears in black, white, or colour depending on context. On packaging and cups, the background colour identifies the product type or size, and the logo appears in white on top of it.

Approved placements

Primary — black on white

Default. Black logo on white or light backgrounds — stationery, editorial, web.

Reversed — white on dark

White logo on espresso, dark brown, or dark product colour backgrounds.

On warm sand (email / print)

Black logo on warm parchment and sand surfaces used in email and print editorial.

Colour — white on product colour

White logo on a product colour background. The colour identifies the coffee type or cup size. See section 04.

Anatomy

Outer ruled border

A thin rectangular frame encloses the entire wordmark. The frame is inseparable from the logo — never use the type alone without it.

Interior rule lines

Thin horizontal rules sit above and below the type stack, inside the outer border. Part of the original artwork — do not redraw.

Condensed wordmark

Bold condensed display type, two lines: ALTEZANO / BROTHERS. Uppercase only. Always use the master artwork file — never typeset from a font.

Clearspace & minimum size

Clearspace rule

Minimum clearspace = height of the cap letters on all four sides of the outer frame. No other elements within this zone.

Minimum sizes

Screen — min 80px wide

Print — min 18mm wide at 300dpi

Don'ts

✕

Don't remove the border frame

The ruled border and interior rules are integral to the logo. Never use the type alone without the enclosing frame — the complete lockup is the logo.

✕

Don't stretch or distort

Always reproduce at original proportions. Never scale width and height independently — the condensed letterforms are designed at a specific ratio.

✕

Don't place on low-contrast backgrounds

Always ensure sufficient contrast between the logo and its background. If placing on photography, use a colour-block or dark overlay to maintain legibility.

✕

Don't recreate from type

Always use the master artwork file. Attempting to recreate it from a similar font will always produce a subtly wrong result.

03

Folk Pattern

The folk decorative band is a signature brand element seen on takeaway cups and packaging. It references the brand's Latin American heritage through two repeating geometric motifs — a rotated diamond and an 8-point starburst flower. The motifs alternate across two rows, with the even row offset by one motif to create a brick-like stagger.

The two motifs

Diamond

8-point flower

The two motifs alternate across two rows. The even row is offset by one motif — where row one has a diamond, row two has a flower, and vice versa — creating a brick-like stagger. The band always runs full-width and is used as a border element at the base of cups and packaging — never as a background fill.

Colour variants

Black on white — stationery, print editorial, light backgrounds

White on espresso — email headers, dark sections

White on product colour — cups & packaging

Cup lockup — complete treatment

Logo above the folk pattern band on a product colour background. The pattern uses the staggered two-row layout — the even row offset by one motif.

Usage rules

Do
Use as a full-width border band at the base (or top) of cups, bags, and print collateral. Always white on dark/product backgrounds; black on light backgrounds.
Do
Maintain the two-row structure with the even row offset by one motif. This stagger is part of the pattern's character and should not be altered.
Don't
Don't use the pattern as a full-bleed background fill or wallpaper repeat. It is a border element — framing, not filling.
Don't
Don't use in any colour combination other than the three approved variants: black on white, white on espresso, or white on product colour.
04

Product Colours

On physical products — cups, bags, and labels — colour is a functional system. It communicates coffee type and cup size at a glance. The logo appears in white on top of the product colour, along with the white folk pattern band. The specific colour-to-product mapping is managed by the production team and may evolve as the range grows.

How it works

The background colour of a cup or bag tells you what's inside — which blend, which origin, or which size. The logo and folk pattern appear on top of the product colour, with the pattern using the characteristic staggered two-row layout.

Product colour examples

The examples below illustrate how colours might be assigned. The actual mapping is confirmed and maintained by the production team as the canonical reference.

#2D5C32Forest green
#1C3C20Deep green
#1A1209Espresso black
Additional colours — defined by production team

Rules

Do
Keep the logo and folk pattern white on product colour backgrounds. The colour is the variable; the white logo treatment is the constant that ties all packaging together.
Do
Let the production team own the colour-to-product mapping. They maintain the canonical reference. If you're unsure which colour maps to which product, check with them before producing assets.
Don't
Don't use product colours as primary colours in email campaigns, web pages, or editorial design. They carry packaging-specific meaning that will be misread in other contexts.
Don't
Don't invent new product colours without consulting the production team. The mapping is deliberate and needs to stay consistent across all physical touchpoints.

Usage rules

Do
Use gold (#C8A96E) sparingly — left-rule accents, the italic gold word in hero headlines, selected chip states. It should feel earned, not scattered.
Don't
Don't use gold as a large background fill, and never apply it to the logo. It is an accent, not a surface colour.
Do
Keep backgrounds within the warm neutral family. #F5F0E8, #FDFAF4 and #F5EDD8 are the three primary surfaces for email and editorial.
Don't
Don't introduce off-palette colours — no blues, purples, or arbitrary teals. The only exception is the organic green (#EAF3DE / #3B6D11), used exclusively for that badge.
Do
Use espresso (#1A1209) and dark roast (#2A1E0F) backgrounds for email headers, footer bands, and section dividers — they create strong visual anchors.
Don't
Don't use pure black (#000) or pure white (#FFF) in email or editorial. Always use the warm equivalents: #1A1209 and #F5F0E8.

Typeface pairing rules

Always

Serif headings, sans body

Playfair Display for all display-size headings. DM Sans for everything below heading level — labels, body, CTAs, metadata. Never use Playfair at small sizes (below 16px).

Always

Italic gold for the accent word

In hero headlines, one word or short phrase becomes italic Playfair in gold (#C8A96E). It's a signature move — once per layout, never more.

Never

Don't mix weights heavily

DM Sans uses three weights: 300 (dark bg body), 400 (body), 500 (labels, CTAs). Never use 600 or 700 — heavy weights feel off-brand.

Never

Don't introduce third typefaces

Playfair + DM Sans is the complete system. No system fonts, no Helvetica as a design choice, no decorative display fonts. The pairing is the identity.

CTA hierarchy

1

Primary — one full-width espresso button

The single dominant action. "Find my coffee →" or "Shop the blends →". Never more than one primary button per email.

2

Secondary — side-by-side outline buttons

"Shop blends" / "Shop single origins". Outline style, placed below the primary CTA after the main content.

3

Text links — footer only

Website, email address, unsubscribe. #7A5C2A with underline. No button treatment.

Technical rules

Do
Use table-based layout with role="presentation" and cellpadding="0" cellspacing="0" on every table. This ensures correct rendering in Outlook.
Don't
Don't use CSS flexbox, grid, or CSS variables in email. All styles must be inline on the element. Never reference a stylesheet from an email.
Do
Include Google Fonts via @import in a <style> block with Helvetica → Arial fallbacks. Outlook desktop ignores web fonts — always design so it works with the fallback active.
Don't
Don't use JavaScript, interactive form elements, or position: fixed in email. None of these are reliably supported across clients.
Do
Always include a preheader — a hidden text element immediately after the body opening that previews the email in the inbox. Keep it under 90 characters and make it earn its place.
Don't
Don't use pure black (#000) or pure white (#FFF) in email. Always use the warm palette equivalents: #1A1209 and #F5F0E8. It renders better and feels more on-brand.

Voice & Copy

We write like a knowledgeable friend who happens to know everything about coffee — precise, warm, and economical. Never corporate, never vague, never breathlessly enthusiastic.

Tone attributes

Precise

Specific details build trust. Name the region, altitude, process, and notes.

"Natural process, 1000–1200m, Ibiraci, Alta Mogiana."
"An incredible coffee with amazing flavour!"

Confident

We know our product. State the experience directly — don't hedge with "might" or "could".

"Best savoured black."
"Might be nice to try without milk."

Warm

Coffee is personal. Community and hospitality are in our DNA — this is a family brand.

"To this day, we source from those same families."

Economical

Every word earns its place. Subject lines, product descriptions and CTAs should be tighter than you think.

"A bright coffee. Complex when enjoyed black."
"This is a really bright and very complex coffee that you will truly enjoy when you drink it without milk."

Unpretentious

Specialty coffee, not snob coffee. Every drinker is welcome.

"Whether you're a café owner or a recreational sipper."

Story-aware

Origin is everything. Connect the coffee to its place and people when there's space.

"These were days of revolution — this is where our passion began."

Copy do / don't

Subject line — do

"Which coffee is yours? Find out in 30 seconds."

Subject line — don't

"🔥 AMAZING new coffees you NEED to try RIGHT NOW!!!"

Product description — do

"A bright coffee, balanced with milk, complex when enjoyed black. Floral and dried fruit — Colombia, Ethiopia, Guatemala and PNG."

Product description — don't

"Our Bold blend is an amazing, incredible coffee that coffee lovers will absolutely adore and love every single sip of."

CTA — do

"Shop the blends →" / "Find my coffee →" / "Shop single origins →"

CTA — don't

"Click here!" / "BUY NOW!!!" / "Get yours today before it's gone!"

Preheader — do

"Three questions. Your perfect cup. Three blends, eight single origins."

Preheader — don't

"Check out our great range of delicious coffees at amazing prices — don't miss out!"

Imagery Direction

Real farms, real people, real process. Warm, documentary, and tactile — never generic, staged, or artificial. Photography should feel like it comes from the same place as the brand itself.

Photography categories

Origin & farm

Origin photographyHigh-altitude farms, harvesting, farmers at origin. Natural documentary light — not styled or directed.

Roastery & process

Roastery processThe Auckland roastery in action. Green beans, roasted beans, the roaster itself. Tactile and real.

Product & packaging

Product shotsBags on warm, textured surfaces — never white. Natural props: burlap, timber, ceramic. Always contextual.

Cup & café moments

Cup & caféCoffee being made and enjoyed. Real people. Warm ambient light — never studio flash or white backgrounds.

Colour grading direction

Correct grading
Warm shadows (brown, not blue-grey). Slightly muted, not oversaturated. Highlights pulling toward parchment. Natural film grain is acceptable.
Avoid
Cool blue-tinted shadows. Heavy HDR or oversaturation. Flat overlit studio white. Heavy vignetting. Instagram-style filters. AI-generated imagery of any kind.

What not to use

✕

No generic stock photography

No one staring lovingly at a latte. No hands cupping a mug on a white table. Imagery must be specific to Altezano Brothers — real places, real people.

✕

No white-background product shots

Bags floating on white feel e-commerce generic. Always photograph on warm, textured surfaces consistent with the brand palette.

✕

No AI-generated imagery

Our brand is built on authenticity and traceability. AI images undermine both. All photography must be real — real places, real people, real process.

✕

No text directly on photography

Don't overlay headline text on photography without a dark overlay or colour-block separator. Legibility always wins over effect.
