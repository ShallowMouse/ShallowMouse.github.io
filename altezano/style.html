Logo & Wordmark

The Altezano Brothers logo is a bold condensed wordmark. It appears in black, white, or colour depending on context. On packaging and cups, the background colour identifies the product type or size.

Approved placements

Primary — black on white

Default. Black logo on white or light backgrounds — stationery, editorial, web.

Reversed — white on dark

White logo on espresso, dark brown, or dark product colour backgrounds.

On warm sand (email / print)

Black logo on warm parchment and sand surfaces used in email and print editorial.

Colour — white on product colour

White logo on a product colour background. The colour identifies the coffee type or cup size. See section 04.

Anatomy

Interior rule lines

Thin horizontal rules sit above and below the type stack. Part of the original artwork — do not redraw.

Condensed wordmark

Bold condensed display type, two lines: ALTEZANO / BROTHERS. Uppercase only. Always use the master artwork file — never typeset from a font.

Clearspace & minimum size

Clearspace rule

Minimum clearspace = height of the cap letters on all four sides of the logo. No other elements within this zone.

Minimum sizes

Screen — min 80px wide

Print — min 18mm wide at 300dpi

Don'ts

✕

Don't stretch or distort

Always reproduce at original proportions. The condensed letterforms are designed at a specific ratio — stretching them changes the character of the type and signals a lack of care about the brand.

✕

Don't place on low-contrast backgrounds

Legibility is the minimum requirement for any logo. If the wordmark can't be read clearly, it fails its purpose entirely. On photography, use a flat colour block or dark overlay to give the logo a clean field to sit on.

✕

Don't recreate from type

Always use the master artwork file. No available typeface exactly matches the original artwork — any recreation will be subtly but unmistakably wrong, and that wrongness compounds at larger sizes where the letterforms are more closely scrutinised.

03

Folk Pattern

The folk decorative band is a signature brand element seen on takeaway cups and packaging. It references the brand's Latin American heritage through two repeating geometric motifs — a rotated diamond and an 8-point starburst flower. The motifs alternate across two rows, with the even row offset by one motif to create a brick-like stagger.

The two motifs

Diamond

8-point flower

The two motifs alternate across two rows. The even row is offset by one motif — where row one has a diamond, row two has a flower, and vice versa — creating a brick-like stagger. The band always runs full-width and is used as a border element at the base of cups and packaging — never as a background fill.

Colour variants

Product colour on white — cups, bags, and packaging with a light surface

Warm sand on espresso — email headers and dark editorial sections

Product colour on cream — the pattern as it appears on natural/uncoated packaging stock

Cup lockup — complete treatment

The folk pattern band printed in brand green on a white cup surface, below the black logo. The motifs stagger across two rows — the even row offset by one motif.

Usage rules

Do
Use as a full-width border band at the base (or top) of cups and bags. Running the full width is what makes it read as a structural trim rather than a decorative stamp — a contained band that grounds the packaging.
Do
Maintain the two-row structure with the even row offset by one motif. The alternating stagger is what gives the pattern its rhythm — a single row or aligned rows lose the visual energy that makes it distinctive.
Don't
Don't use as a full-bleed background fill or wallpaper repeat. Repeated at scale, the geometric motifs become visual noise — the pattern's character depends on the contrast between a dense contained band and the plain surface around it.
04

Product Colours

On physical products — cups, bags, and labels — colour is a functional system. It communicates coffee type and cup size at a glance. The logo and folk pattern appear on the product colour surface. The specific colour-to-product mapping is managed by the production team and may evolve as the range grows.

How it works

The background colour of a cup or bag tells you what's inside — which blend, which origin, or which size. The product colour is applied to the cup or bag. The logo and folk pattern are printed in a contrasting colour on that surface, with the pattern using the staggered two-row layout.

Product colour examples

The examples below illustrate how colours might be assigned. The actual mapping is confirmed and maintained by the production team as the canonical reference.

#2D5C32Forest green
#1C3C20Deep green
#1A1209Espresso black
Additional colours — defined by production team

Rules

Do
The product colour is applied to the cup or bag surface. The logo and pattern are printed on top in a legible contrasting colour. The colour is the signal — the logo and pattern are the constants that identify the brand regardless of which product you're holding.
Do
Let the production team own the colour-to-product mapping and check with them before producing new assets. Customers learn to read the colour — an undocumented change breaks that learned association and makes the system unreliable.
Don't
Don't use product colours in email, web, or print editorial. On a cup, green means "this specific blend." In an email, it carries no such meaning — or worse, implies something unintended and confuses the reader.
Don't
Don't introduce new product colours without the production team. Adding a colour without updating the canonical reference means other designers won't know what it signals — and a coding system only works if every stakeholder reads it the same way.

Usage rules

Do
Use gold (#C8A96E) sparingly — left-rule accents, the italic hero word, selected chip states. Gold's value comes from its rarity in the layout. Used frequently it becomes background noise; reserved for one or two moments it signals something worth noticing.
Don't
Don't use gold as a large background fill. At scale it is warm but assertive — it competes with everything around it and undermines the calm, editorial feel the brand depends on.
Do
Keep backgrounds within the warm neutral family — #F5F0E8, #FDFAF4 and #F5EDD8. The three surfaces create a layered hierarchy without visual competition. Introducing other background colours fractures the palette and breaks the warmth that defines the aesthetic.
Don't
Don't introduce off-palette colours — no blues, purples, or arbitrary teals. Every colour in the system should feel like it belongs to the same warm, coffee-derived world. Cool colours signal a different brand entirely. The only exception is the pale organic green (#EAF3DE / #3B6D11), which is confined to the certified organic badge.
Do
Use espresso (#1A1209) and dark roast (#2A1E0F) backgrounds for email headers, footer bands, and section dividers. Strong dark anchors at the top and bottom of a layout give the warm cream sections structure and intention — without them, the layout drifts.
Don't
Don't use pure black (#000) or pure white (#FFF) in email or editorial. They are optical extremes that read as cold and clinical against a warm palette. The espresso and parchment equivalents carry the same contrast but stay within the brand's temperature.

Typeface pairing rules

Always

Serif headings, sans body

Playfair Display at large sizes carries personality and warmth. At body size, it becomes dense and effortful to read. DM Sans handles the functional load cleanly, letting Playfair do its job where it counts — large, editorial, expressive. Never use Playfair below 16px.

Always

Italic gold for the accent word

One word in the hero headline becomes italic Playfair in gold (#C8A96E) — creating a focal point and a moment of warmth. Used more than once per layout, the effect collapses. It only works because it's singular.

Never

Don't use weights above 500

DM Sans at 600 or 700 reads as assertive and corporate — at odds with the brand's warm, refined tone. The three weights in use (300 for dark-bg body, 400 for body, 500 for labels and CTAs) cover every hierarchy need without hardening the aesthetic.

Never

Don't introduce third typefaces

Every additional typeface introduces visual noise and dilutes the identity. Playfair and DM Sans cover every communication need from hero headline to fine print. A third font always looks like an accident — or a brand that doesn't know what it is.

Anatomy & usage rules

Border: 1px solid #C8A96E top and bottom. Vertical dividers also 1px solid #C8A96E.

Values: Playfair Display 32px / weight 400 / line-height 1.15. All values use the same size and colour regardless of content length — this keeps the bar visually balanced across columns.

Labels: DM Sans 10px / weight 500 / uppercase / letter-spacing 0.18em / --c-muted. All labels use the same size and colour — consistent treatment reinforces the grid structure.

Backgrounds: Works on both light (card/page) and dark (espresso) backgrounds. On dark backgrounds, values use parchment (#F5F0E8) and labels use --c-muted, with the flavour label remaining gold.

Column proportions: The centre column uses a 1fr 2fr 1fr grid — giving the flavour profile twice the width of the flanking data points. This keeps flavour descriptions on a single line and signals that the centre carries the most descriptive content. The outer columns hold shorter values (numbers, place names) that need less room.

Use cases: Product detail pages, email product sections, print packaging inserts, wholesale catalogue.

Technical rules

Do
Use table-based layout with role="presentation" and cellpadding="0" cellspacing="0" on every table. Outlook — which handles a large share of business email — ignores modern CSS layout entirely. Tables are the only method guaranteed to render correctly across all major clients.
Don't
Don't use CSS flexbox, grid, or CSS variables in email — all styles must be inline on the element. Email clients use stripped-down rendering engines, not full browsers. What looks correct in Chrome can break entirely in Outlook or older mobile clients.
Do
Include Google Fonts via @import in a <style> block, with Helvetica → Arial fallbacks. Most email clients load web fonts, but Outlook desktop does not. If the design doesn't work in Helvetica and Arial, it will break for a significant portion of recipients.
Don't
Don't use JavaScript, interactive form elements, or position: fixed in email. JavaScript is stripped for security. Form elements rarely function. And position: fixed collapses inside email viewports because the iframe has no fixed reference point.
Do
Always include a preheader — a hidden text element after the body opening tag that appears in the inbox preview. It's the second thing a recipient reads after the subject line, and it determines whether they open the email. Without one, the client pulls in whatever comes first — often an unsubscribe link.
Don't
Don't use pure black (#000) or pure white (#FFF) in email. They render as cold and generic — immediately at odds with the warm palette. #1A1209 and #F5F0E8 carry the same functional contrast while keeping every element within the brand's temperature.

Email template types

Four template types cover the full range of Altezano Brothers email communications. Each assembles the blocks above in a specific order with a clear primary objective.

Welcome email

First contact. One job.

Sent immediately after signup. Introduces the brand story, sets the tone, and drives the reader to the coffee picker or the range. No discounts — the product is the hook.

Structure: Dark hero → brand story paragraph → coffee picker CTA → routing band → footer.

Promotional email

Range, seasonal, or offer.

New arrivals, seasonal lots, or occasional offers. Light hero, 2–4 product blocks, one primary CTA. Never more than one offer per email — specificity wins.

Structure: Light hero → product blocks (2-col grid or stacked) → primary CTA → testimonial → footer.

Product launch

One coffee. All the detail.

Dedicated to a single new or featured coffee. Uses the fact bar, full tasting notes, origin story, and process detail. The most editorial template.

Structure: Light hero (product name + italic note) → fact bar → origin copy → product block → primary CTA → related products → footer.

Transactional

Order confirmed. Keep it brief.

Order confirmation, dispatch notification, and subscription updates. Functional and warm — the brand tone holds even here. No promotions in transactional emails.

Structure: Dark header with logo → confirmation message → order summary table → dispatch info → footer. No product blocks, no CTAs beyond order tracking.

Subject line & preheader patterns

Subject lines should be precise, warm, and under 50 characters where possible — most mobile clients cut at around 40. Preheaders extend the subject without repeating it, and should add information rather than summarise.

Character limits: Subject line — aim for 40–50 characters. Preheader — 85–100 characters. Avoid: ALL CAPS, excessive punctuation (!!!), and emoji in subject lines. Dark mode

Approximately 35% of email opens occur in dark mode. Apple Mail and iOS Mail force invert colours unless you explicitly control them. The warm palette is particularly vulnerable — espresso can flip to near-white, parchment to near-black.

Protect these elements

Add color-scheme: light to the <html> and <meta> tags to signal light-mode preference.

Use @media (prefers-color-scheme: dark) in the <style> block to set explicit dark overrides.

Set explicit background colours on every <td> — never rely on inherited background in dark mode.

Logo images: use the SVG or PNG version with a transparent background so they invert naturally with the surrounding surface.

Mobile email

Over 60% of Altezano Brothers email opens are on mobile. The 600px fixed-width email card should render correctly at any viewport width down to 320px. Two-column layouts must stack to single column below 480px.

Required mobile behaviours

Set width: 100% !important on the outer wrapper <table> in a <style> block — the 600px card collapses to full-width on small screens.

Two-column <td> pairs: use display: block; width: 100% below 480px so cards stack vertically.

Reduce horizontal padding from 48px to 24px on mobile. Section padding: 24–28px on mobile vs 32–40px on desktop.

Minimum tap target: 44px height on all buttons and links. Primary CTAs should be full-width on mobile.

Hero heading: reduce from 36–42px to 28px on mobile. Never let the hero heading wrap to more than 3 lines.

Voice & Copy

We write like a knowledgeable friend who happens to know everything about coffee — precise, warm, and economical. Never corporate, never vague, never breathlessly enthusiastic.

Tone attributes

Precise

Specific details build trust. Name the region, altitude, process, and notes.

"Natural process, 1000–1200m, Ibiraci, Alta Mogiana."
"An incredible coffee with amazing flavour!"

Confident

We know our product. State the experience directly — don't hedge with "might" or "could".

"Best savoured black."
"Might be nice to try without milk."

Warm

Coffee is personal. Community and hospitality are in our DNA — this is a family brand.

"To this day, we source from those same families."

Economical

Every word earns its place. Subject lines, product descriptions and CTAs should be tighter than you think.

"A bright coffee. Complex when enjoyed black."
"This is a really bright and very complex coffee that you will truly enjoy when you drink it without milk."

Unpretentious

Specialty coffee, not snob coffee. Every drinker is welcome.

"Whether you're a café owner or a recreational sipper."

Story-aware

Origin is everything. Connect the coffee to its place and people when there's space.

"These were days of revolution — this is where our passion began."

Copy do / don't

Subject line — do

"Which coffee is yours? Find out in 30 seconds."

Subject line — don't

"🔥 AMAZING new coffees you NEED to try RIGHT NOW!!!"

Product description — do

"A bright coffee, balanced with milk, complex when enjoyed black. Floral and dried fruit — Colombia, Ethiopia, Guatemala and Papua New Guinea."

Product description — don't

"Our Bold blend is an amazing, incredible coffee that coffee lovers will absolutely adore and love every single sip of."

CTA — do

"Shop the blends →" / "Find my coffee →" / "Shop single origins →"

CTA — don't

"Click here!" / "BUY NOW!!!" / "Get yours today before it's gone!"

Preheader — do

"Three questions. Your perfect cup. Three blends, eight single origins."

Preheader — don't

"Check out our great range of delicious coffees at amazing prices — don't miss out!"

Imagery Direction

Real farms, real people, real process. Warm, documentary, and tactile — never generic, staged, or artificial. Photography should feel like it comes from the same place as the brand itself.

Photography categories

Origin & farm

Origin photographyHigh-altitude farms, harvesting, farmers at origin. Natural documentary light — not styled or directed.

Roastery & process

Roastery processThe Auckland roastery in action. Green beans, roasted beans, the roaster itself. Tactile and real.

Product & packaging

Product shotsBags on warm, textured surfaces — never white. Natural props: burlap, timber, ceramic. Always contextual.

Cup & café moments

Cup & caféCoffee being made and enjoyed. Real people. Warm ambient light — never studio flash or white backgrounds.

Colour grading direction

Correct grading
Warm shadows (brown, not blue-grey), slightly muted saturation, highlights pulling toward parchment. This grading keeps photography within the same temperature as the brand palette — so images feel like they belong rather than feeling pasted in from a different world.
Avoid
Cool blue-tinted shadows, heavy HDR, oversaturation, overlit studio white, heavy vignetting, and Instagram-style filters. All of these introduce either a coolness or an artifice that conflicts with the brand's warmth and authenticity. AI-generated imagery is never acceptable — the brand is built on real places, real people, and real relationships.

What not to use

✕

No generic stock photography

Audiences recognise stock imagery instantly — it has a visual signature that signals inauthenticity. For a brand built on direct relationships and traceability, stock photography contradicts everything we claim to stand for.

✕

No white-background product shots

White backgrounds read as catalogue or marketplace — context-free and interchangeable with any other product. The brand's aesthetic depends on warmth and context. A bag on natural burlap tells a story; a bag on white tells nothing.

✕

No AI-generated imagery

The brand's entire identity rests on real relationships with real farmers in real places. AI imagery is, by definition, not real — using it directly contradicts every claim the brand makes about origin, traceability, and authenticity.

✕

No text directly on photography

Photography already competes for attention — overlaying type without a clear separator forces the eye to choose between two things at once and usually fails at both. A flat colour block lets the type breathe and the image hold its own.
